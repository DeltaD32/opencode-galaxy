## Voice - What We Say

The voice serves as the basis for communication by BMW Group applications. With BMW Group voice we help empowering users to meet their business goals. The voice reflects the personality of the BMW Group.

## Tone - How We Say Things

Tone conveys an attitude toward both, the subject matter and the reader. Always keep your target audience in mind. Adjust the style and tone to the situation.

Be casual in guiding and enlightening the users. Don't be afraid to be funny, but don't be silly.

---

## Characteristics

### 1. Concise

To facilitate navigation and discovery, write UI text in short, scannable segments. The focus of the text should be limited to a small number of concepts at a time.

**Do:**

> "Request your access. It's fast and easy."

**Don't:**

> "Request (and approve) one or more authorization roles to enter the productive environment. The three-step process is guided by detailed instructions and you are able to access the system soon."

---

### 2. Simple and Direct

Use simple language that makes content easy to understand. Ensure that all users are familiar with the terms used. Talk directly - don't beat about the bush.

**Do:**

> "Save changes?"

**Don't:**

> "Would you like to save your changes?"

---

### 3. Active and Passive Voice

Basically, the active voice should be used. Always remember that you want to encourage users to interact with the application. Avoid passive voice when possible.

**Do:**

> "You saved the request successfully."

**Don't:**

> "The request was saved by you successfully."

---

### 4. Positive Writing

Use positive language rather than negative language. One way to detect negative language is to look for words like "don't" or "can't".

**Do:**

> "Choose a role and send the request."

**Don't:**

> "You can't send the request when you don't enter a role."

---

### 5. Tenses

Use the present tense to describe product behavior. Avoid using the future tense to describe the way a product always acts, just stick to simple present. When you need to write in the past or future tenses, use the simple past or will-future.

**Do:**

> "Request sent."

**Don't:**

> "Request has been sent."

---

## Perspective

Address users in either the second person (you or your) or the first person (I, me, or my), depending on which is suitable and clearest for the situation.

### 1. Second Person

Engage your users by using second person. This conversational style is appropriate in most situations; as though the UI is speaking directly to the user. Use "you" or "your" when your product is asking questions, giving instructions, or describing things to the users. Just imagine what a personal assistant might say.

**Do:**

> "You sent the request successfully."

**Don't:**

> "Transfer of the request was successful."

---

### 2. First Person

You can use first person in headings or labels that are very specific to the user or customer data. In some cases, this form of address emphasizes the user's ownership of content or actions. "My" feels personal. It feels like you can customize and control it.

**Do:**

> My Account

**Don't:**

> User Account

---

### 3. Third Person

By using "we", "our" or "us" you refer to the people behind the product. "We" adds a human touch. Knowing that real humans are there behind all those windows and boxes, can help the user feel a little more at ease.

**Do:**

> "See our confluence page."

**Don't:**

> "Product confluence page."
